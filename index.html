Climate Risks Across U.S. National Parks

This coordinated geovisualization combines National Park Service information with monthly NOAA climate indicators from 1985 to 2026.

Use the map to explore climate risk patterns, compare parks through the ranked bar chart, and inspect individual parks with NPS descriptions and images.

How to use

  1. Select a climate risk indicator.
  2. Move the year slider.
  3. Filter parks by minimum risk.
  4. Click a park or ranking bar to inspect details.
  5. Use quick zoom buttons for Mainland U.S., Alaska, Hawaii, and Virgin Islands.

Story + About

National parks protect iconic landscapes, ecosystems, and cultural resources, but these places are increasingly exposed to climate stress.

Project Story

U.S. national parks are often imagined as protected spaces, yet protection from development does not mean protection from climate change. Temperature extremes, drought, and precipitation variability can affect vegetation, wildfire conditions, water resources, visitor safety, and long-term conservation planning.

This project combines a story-driven interface with an exploratory mapping tool. The map supports free exploration, while the ranking chart helps users compare parks systematically across risk indicators and years.

Target User

The intended user is an environmental planner or climate adaptation practitioner who needs to identify high-risk parks, compare regional patterns, and communicate climate-related vulnerability to public audiences.

Use Scenario

A planner opens the explorer, selects a risk indicator, moves through historical years, filters parks by risk threshold, and compares the top-ranked parks through linked map and chart views.

Interaction Design

The interface supports search, pan and zoom, reexpression by indicator, temporal exploration, risk filtering, linked highlighting, and park-level detail retrieval.

Design Strategy

The map emphasizes spatial pattern recognition, while the ranked bar chart emphasizes ordered comparison. Fixed-size points avoid confusing symbol size with risk magnitude.

Conclusion

Climate risk across national parks is regionally uneven and hazard-specific.

Emerging Spatial Patterns

Western parks often show stronger heat and drought stress

Parks in the Southwest and interior West tend to rank higher for heat and drought-related indicators. This pattern likely reflects arid climate regimes, high summer temperatures, and limited water availability.

Island parks require separate interpretation

Hawaii and Virgin Islands parks are geographically small and distant from the mainland, but they should not be visually minimized. Their climate exposure can involve heat, precipitation extremes, and coastal vulnerability.

Alaska parks show distinct climate signals

Alaska parks may not always rank highest under drought indicators, but they are important for interpreting temperature change in high-latitude protected landscapes.

Risk is not one-dimensional

A park with high heat risk may not have high precipitation risk. This is why the dropdown separates overall risk, heat stress, drought stress, and heavy precipitation instead of using only one general climate score.

Interpretive Takeaway

The goal of the application is not to claim a single “most threatened” park. Instead, it helps users identify which parks stand out under different climate stressors and supports region-specific adaptation thinking.

Data + Methods

The application joins National Park Service park information with monthly climate indicators using parkCode.

Data Sources

  • National Park Service API: park names, coordinates, descriptions, states, designations, images, and official URLs.
  • NOAA monthly climate data: park-level monthly temperature, maximum temperature, minimum temperature, precipitation, and drought index from 1985 to 2026.

Join Method

Park attributes from the NPS API are joined to the climate table using parkCode. Monthly records are aggregated to annual park-level values.

Risk Calculation

Heat stress risk is derived from maximum temperature, drought stress risk is derived from the drought index, and heavy precipitation risk is derived from annual precipitation. These values are normalized to a 0–100 relative scale for comparison. The overall climate risk score combines heat, drought, and precipitation indicators.
